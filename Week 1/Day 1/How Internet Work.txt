How Internet Work?
1. Devices connect to a network
    Your phone, laptop, or computer connects to the internet using:
    Wi-Fi (via a router)
    Mobile data (via cell towers)
    Wired connection (Ethernet cable)

2. Router & ISP (Internet Service Provider)
    Your device sends requests to your router.
    The router forwards your request to your ISP (like Airtel, Jio, BSNL, etc. in India).
    The ISP gives you access to the global internet.

3. IP Address
    Every device on the internet has a unique identifier called an IP address (like a phone number for devices).
    Your ISP assigns you an IP address so that websites and other devices know where to send information.

4. DNS (Domain Name System)
    You type www.google.com into your browser.
    Computers donâ€™t understand names, they understand IP addresses.
    DNS works like a phonebook, converting the website name into the websiteâ€™s IP address (e.g., 142.250.190.14).

5. Data travels in packets
    Your request (â€œopen google.comâ€) is broken into tiny data packets.
    These packets travel through cables, satellites, undersea fiber optic lines, and networks across the world.
    They find the fastest route to reach the destination (like traffic navigation).

6. Servers respond
    The server where the website is hosted receives your request.
    It processes it and sends back the website data (also in packets).

7. Reassembling & Display
    Your browser collects all the packets, reassembles them, and displays the website for you.

This all happens in milliseconds!

ğŸŒ What is Routing?
    Routing is the process of finding the best path for data packets to travel across networks (from source to destination).
    Every device on the internet (like your phone, laptop, or a server) has an IP address. Routers are responsible for directing packets between these devices.

ğŸ›  How Routing Works in the Internet
    Packet Creation
    Data is broken into packets.
    Each packet contains: Source IP, Destination IP, and Data.
    Routers Forward Packets
    A router checks the destination IP.
    It uses a routing table (a list of known networks and next hops) to decide where to send the packet.
    Multiple Hops
    A packet usually travels through multiple routers (called hops).
    Each hop brings it closer to the destination.
    Reaching the Destination
    Finally, the packet reaches the target device (like a web server).
    The server responds with packets that follow the reverse route (not always the exact same path).
